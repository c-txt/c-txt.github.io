Peter's Folly, Paul's Difficulty

Abstract: When Peter and the other apostles presented Matthias as a replacement for Judas' apostleship, they did not realize that they were making an error. It can be easily surmised that the blunder would later lead to difficulties for the Lord's chosen replacement, Paul. All this goes to show that, rightly dividing the scriptures requires not merely the intellect but the guidance of the Holy Spirit.

A decision that just felt right

The last chapter in the gospel of John records the account of the Lord Jesus lovingly restoring Peter. But then the book of Acts begins with a record of another one of Peter's blunders. It is recorded for us in Acts 1: 15-26.

In those days Peter stood up among the brothers (the company of persons was in all about 120)

Peter was an impulsive man, a natural leader, who would not only speak what was on his mind but rush in where others would fear to tread. Earlier, he had cut off Malchus' ear, and he had dared to even walk on turbulent waters. His inclination was to speak or act first and think only later. And here was that same spirit in action.

and said, “Brothers, the Scripture had to be fulfilled, which the Holy Spirit spoke beforehand by the mouth of David concerning Judas, who became a guide to those who arrested Jesus. For he was numbered among us and was allotted his share in this ministry.”

“For it is written in the Book of Psalms, “‘May his camp become desolate, and let there be no one to dwell in it’; and “‘Let another take his office.’

Here was Peter trying to understand the inspired scriptures using his own mind. And that unfortunately was the problem even for one who was personally chosen by the Lord to be one of His apostles. As we'll see in a moment, he remembered some things but forgot the key command that was most recently delivered by the Lord.

His initial reasonings were valid, for had not the Lord said earlier that the apostles would sit on twelve thrones in His kingdom judging the twelve tribes of Israel. And would not one of those thrones remain vacant, now that Judas was dead? And did not the Psalms say that another should take his office?

But then the plot began to go wrong. Peter decided that it was time to set things right himself, and got the disciples all thinking.

So one of the men who have accompanied us during all the time that the Lord Jesus went in and out among us, beginning from the baptism of John until the day when he was taken up from us—one of these men must become with us a witness to his resurrection.”

And they put forward two, Joseph called Barsabbas, who was also called Justus, and Matthias.

And they prayed and said, “You, Lord, who know the hearts of all, show which one of these two you have chosen to take the place in this ministry and apostleship from which Judas turned aside to go to his own place.”

And they cast lots for them, and the lot fell on Matthias

A choice that was not so clearly wrong

So was Matthais' election really a blunder? Did the other apostles not follow sound principles of electing a replacement for Judas? Did not Proverbs 16:33 also say that every decision of a lot was from the hand of the Lord? It would appear that the disciples led by Peter were making a wise, and scriptural decison that perhaps in their minds would hasten the coming of the kingdom to Israel. However, Luke, the author of the book of Acts records the sorry outcome.

and he (Matthias) was numbered with the eleven apostles.

Note that Luke did not say that Matthias was numbered among the twelve apostles. All that happened was that the other disciples accepted the decision of the lot, and counted Matthais as an apostle, but the inspired scriptures still held that there were only eleven apostles even after the choice. Acts 2:14 reinforces the fact of the error.

A few chapters later, Acts 9:6, 15 records that man's choice was not God's choice. The Lord met Paul on the road to Damascus and commissioned him with his apostleship. The final word on the decision ultimately comes in Revelation 21:14. The gates of heavenly Jerusalem will have only twelve names of the twelve apostles. And since, Paul is clearly the Lord's directly appointed final apostle, it is easily concluded that Mathias was never in the eyes of scripture an apostle. It was not Peter's or the other apsotle's job to commission a replacement apostle, and before we understand why they erred, we could surmise for a moment how the blunder might have made things for the genuine replacement.

The consequences of bad hiring

Unfortunately, the decision made by Peter and the other disciples to take matters into their own hands could not have been without consequences.

The great apostle Paul had to struggle throughout his ministry to prove his apostleship. It could be inferred, in the light of the hasty decision by the other apostles, and the numbering of Mathias with the eleven, that there would have been widespread uncertainity among the growing numbers of disciples in the early church, about who really was Judas' replacement.

Nine of Paul's letters bear this out well. In them, he often begins with statements highlighting that it was God's will that he be an apostle. And in the letter to the Galations, he has to stress really hard about the question, going to great lengths to outline that he was not man's choice, but God's, probably referring back to Mathias' selection.

Clearly, if this was the case in his letters, Paul's daily ministry of teaching and preaching and revealing the mysteries of the church that were specificially entrusted only to him among all the other apostles, would have been no easy ride.

A teaching and decision that was not led by the Spirit

So what really was the root cause of all this confusion? In their decision to choose a replacement for Judas, Peter and the disciples were probably still caught up in the kingdom plan, which they assumed would play out in their lifetimes. They also forgot that prior to His ascension, the Lord had given them a key instruction, as recorded in Acts 1:4-5

And while staying with them he ordered them not to depart from Jerusalem, but to wait for the promise of the Father, which, he said, “you heard from me; for John baptized with water, but you will be baptized with the Holy Spirit not many days from now.” Acts 1:4

When Peter stood up to present his plan, he was full of his own spirit, and had forgotten the Lord's clear command to wait for the Holy Spirit. He must have also forgotten the Lord's earlier proimise in John 14: 25-26, while He was still with them, that He would not leave them directionless:

These things I have spoken to you while I am still with you. But the Helper, the Holy Spirit, whom the Father will send in my name, he will teach you all things and bring to your remembrance all that I have said to you.

And perhaps Peter being a man of action even grew tired of all the praying and the waiting, and that we understand only too well because how often we too are like Peter.

Lessons from the goof-up

Despite its consequences to Paul, in retrospect, Peter's blunder offers us many learnings and applications for our own lives and decisions.

Firstly, we must remember that we need patience and the Holy Spirit's help to rightly understand and rightly divide the scriptures. If he had waited, Peter would have understood that the Holy Spirit could come only fifty days after the feast of the passover, as foreshadowed by the feast of the Pentecost. Using their own minds and reasonings, he and the other disciples had misapplied the old testament scriptures, growing impatient and forgetting to obey and depend. In today's age as well, there are many debatable matters where we would do well to wait rather than rush in to conclude. God, the Holy Spirit in his own time will make things clear as we patiently wait on Him.

Second, Peter wrongly applied the scriptures he quoted to the situation at the time, forgetting that while all scripture is for us to learn from, all of it is not for us to apply. Sure, the chair of Judas was empty, but it was not his role to decide who would occupy it. Like him, our fallen minds too are often prone to incorrectly applying the scriptures, which is why a good admonition to remember would be that from Proverbs 3:5 which commends not leaning on our own understanding. Today, the Lord has gifted His church with pastors and teachers able to rightly divide the Word and teach us truth. On the other hand there are many false teachers2 as well, as almost every epistle in the new testament reminds us. So here too, we need to wait on the Holy Spirit to discern sound teaching and instruction.

This bring us to the third learning. Though Peter had a charismatic personality and been in the inner circle of the Lord's disciples, the others should not have blindly followed his suggestion. They should have reminded him of the Lord's commandment to wait. It is not wrong to remember that even the leading men of God are ultimately just fallible, fallen men when not filled by the Spirit. In Galatians 2:11, Paul publically opposed Peter to his face when he was playing the hypocrite, and we too must not be afraid to stand up to our leaders when they are clearly in the wrong.

Lastly, we must not limit God to our choices when making our decisions. The disciples were undoubtedly convinced they were doing the right thing when they chose two candidates for the office and then cast lots. Weren't they allowing the Lord to decide? However, they were in effect limiting all the available options avalable to God. They had applied their own criteria, not knowing that the Lord could commission a replacement apostle directly from heaven and did not really need their help. Often, this is the case with our present day decision-making too. We present our pre-selected, acceptable options to the Lord and pray that He would bless one of those choices. We forget that decision making ought to be led primarily by the Spirit who can lead us into a far better outcome of God's own choice, just as He was preparing the as-yet unknown Paul to take his place among the apostles.

The bottomline

Just like the Lord continued to be gracious towards an imperfect servant like Peter, later filling Him with His Spirit and going on to use him mightily despite his weaknesses and blunders. In the same way, he is gracious towards us when we blunder as well, always being ready to give us of His own Holy Spirit. Yet, because our only-too-human blunders will have unforeseen negative consequences, we must remember to constantly depend on and be filled with the Holy Spirit's guidance. We must learn to walk in step with Him, neither rushing forward, nor straying behind His leading.

Peter eventually acknowledged the wisdom of God's choice. In 2 Peter 3: 15 he said of Paul that he was a beloved brother and had a special wisdom given to him. And then while writing the next verse 16 about the scriptures, mentioning that they are hard to understand, he must have been reminded of his own earlier blunder:

There are some things in them that are hard to understand, which the ignorant and unstable twist to their own destruction, as they do the other Scriptures.

So in the end, whether it be in understanding the scriptures, or in making decisions based on them, let us neither be ignorant nor unstable, but lean heavily on the wisdom and counsel of the Spirit promised by the Father to His children. Amen.
